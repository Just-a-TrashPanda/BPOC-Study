TITLE 1

Introductory Provisions

Purposes & Objectives of the Code
Ch. 1

Chapter 1 states why the Penal Code exists: to establish clear boundaries between criminal and noncriminal conduct, define culpable mental states, prescribe fair warning of what's prohibited, and give courts consistent, proportionate punishment ranges. It also sets territorial jurisdiction — Texas has jurisdiction if any element of the offense (the conduct or the result) occurs inside the state.

Key Statutory Definitions
§1.07

The Code's master definitions section. Distinguishes felony (an offense so designated by law, or punishable by death or imprisonment) from misdemeanor (an offense punishable only by fine, jail, or both). Also defines terms used throughout the code: "bodily injury," "serious bodily injury," "deadly weapon," "deception," "effective consent," "possession," "public place," and "individual" vs. "person" (a "person" can include a corporation or association; an "individual" means a human being).

Burden of Proof
Ch. 2

Establishes the presumption of innocence and requires the State to prove every element of an offense beyond a reasonable doubt. Distinguishes elements of the offense (State's burden) from affirmative defenses, which the defendant must prove by a preponderance of the evidence (a lower standard than beyond a reasonable doubt).

Multiple Prosecutions
Ch. 3

Governs when offenses arising from the same criminal episode can be consolidated into a single trial, and interacts with double-jeopardy protections (which actually derive from the U.S. and Texas Constitutions, not this chapter) to limit multiple punishments for the same conduct.

TITLE 2

General Principles of Criminal Responsibility

Culpable Mental States (Mens Rea)
§6.02–6.03

Almost every offense requires proof of one of four mental states, ranked from most to least culpable:

Intentional — conscious objective or desire to engage in the conduct or cause the result.
Knowing — aware of the nature of the conduct/circumstances, or aware the conduct is reasonably certain to cause the result.
Reckless — aware of but consciously disregards a substantial and unjustifiable risk; a gross deviation from how an ordinary person would act.
Criminal Negligence — should have been aware of a substantial and unjustifiable risk but wasn't; failing to perceive it is a gross deviation from ordinary care.

If a statute defining an offense doesn't specify a mental state, one is still required — intent, knowledge, or recklessness suffices unless the statute plainly dispenses with a mental state.

Parties to Offenses
Ch. 7

A person is criminally responsible for an offense committed by another's conduct if they act with intent to promote or assist the commission of the offense and solicit, encourage, direct, aid, or attempt to aid the other person. This is the "law of parties" — it lets someone be convicted for a crime they didn't personally commit if they helped bring it about. Under §7.02(b), a party can even be responsible for a second, unintended felony if it was committed in furtherance of the conspiracy and should have been anticipated.

General Defenses to Criminal Responsibility
Ch. 8

Circumstances that can excuse or negate criminal responsibility even though the conduct occurred:

Mistake of fact — a reasonable belief about a fact that negates the required mental state.
Mistake of law — a narrow defense limited to reasonable reliance on an official statement of the law later found wrong.
Intoxication — voluntary intoxication is not a defense to the commission of an offense, though it can be relevant at punishment.
Duress — compulsion by threat of imminent death or serious bodily injury to oneself or another.
Entrapment — law enforcement induced the offense through persuasion likely to cause an otherwise law-abiding person to commit it (merely providing an opportunity is not entrapment).
Age — no criminal responsibility for conduct committed by a child younger than 10 (10–16 generally proceed through the juvenile system).
Insanity — as a result of severe mental disease/defect, the actor did not know their conduct was wrong.

Justification Excluding Criminal Responsibility
Ch. 9

Unlike Chapter 8 defenses, a justification says the conduct itself was legally right under the circumstances — the core self-defense and use-of-force chapter for TCOLE purposes:

Necessity (§9.22) — conduct is justified when it's immediately necessary to avoid a greater harm, and a reasonable person would agree.
Self-Defense (§9.31–9.32) — force is justified when and to the degree the actor reasonably believes it's immediately necessary to protect against another's use or attempted use of unlawful force; deadly force adds further conditions (reasonable belief deadly force is immediately necessary to protect against death, serious bodily injury, or certain violent felonies).
Defense of Third Person (§9.33) — stands in the shoes of the person being defended.
Defense of Property (§9.41–9.43) — force (and, in limited circumstances, deadly force) to protect one's own or another's property from trespass or theft.
Law Enforcement's Use of Force (§9.51) — an officer making an arrest or search is justified in using force (and, in narrower circumstances, deadly force) to the degree reasonably believed necessary, subject to specific conditions the academy will cover in depth.

TITLE 3

Punishments

Base punishment ranges (Ch. 12): Class C — fine up to $500 only. Class B — up to 180 days jail and/or up to $2,000. Class A — up to 1 year jail and/or up to $4,000. State Jail Felony — 180 days to 2 years state jail, plus up to $10,000 fine. 3rd Degree Felony — 2–10 years, plus up to $10,000 fine. 2nd Degree Felony — 2–20 years, plus up to $10,000 fine. 1st Degree Felony — 5–99 years or life, plus up to $10,000 fine. Capital Felony — life without parole, or death.
Chapter 12 also sets out the general enhancement provisions that can raise punishment above these base ranges — repeat/habitual offender rules, deadly weapon findings, bias motivation, and more. See the General Enhancements tab for the full list.
CH. 12

General Punishment Enhancements

Repeat & Habitual Felony Offender
§12.42

Applies when a defendant on trial for a felony has one or more prior, sequential felony convictions (state jail priors generally excluded from triggering these bumps).

One prior felony: 3rd degree → punished as 2nd degree; 2nd degree → punished as 1st degree; 1st degree → 15–99 yrs/life. Two sequential prior felonies (any degree except state jail, on trial for any felony except state jail): 25–99 yrs/life.

Repeat Misdemeanor Offender
§12.43

A prior Class A/B conviction (or any felony) raises the minimum on a new Class A or B charge.

Class A with a qualifying prior: 90-day minimum confinement. Class B with a qualifying prior: 30-day minimum confinement.

Deadly Weapon Finding
§1.07(a)(17); Art. 42A.054 CCP

A firearm, or anything manifestly designed for or capable of causing death/serious bodily injury in the manner used, that the actor used or exhibited (or knew a party would use) during the offense or immediate flight.

Doesn't change the offense grade directly, but is entered as an affirmative finding that restricts parole eligibility and community supervision, and can bump a state jail felony under §12.35(c) to a 3rd degree felony.

Bias or Prejudice Motivation
§12.47

An affirmative finding that the offense was committed because of bias or prejudice against a group.

Raises punishment to the next higher offense category (does not apply to 1st degree felonies or Class A misdemeanors, which are already the top of their class).

Drug-Facilitated Offense
§12.49

An affirmative finding that a controlled substance was administered to the victim of a robbery, theft, or offense against the person.

Raises punishment to the next higher offense category.

Disaster / Evacuation Area
§12.50

Assault, robbery, burglary, or theft committed within an area under a declared disaster or emergency evacuation order.

Raises punishment to the next higher category; for a Class A misdemeanor, raises the minimum confinement to 180 days instead of changing the grade.

Weapon-Free School Zone
§46.11

A Chapter 46 weapons offense committed in a place the actor knew was within 300 feet of a school, at a school function, or at a UIL event.

Raises punishment to the next higher offense category.

TITLE 4

Inchoate Offenses

Criminal Attempt
§15.01
−1 category

With specific intent to commit an offense, doing an act amounting to more than mere preparation that tends but fails to bring about the offense. It's no defense that the completed offense was actually committed.

Punished one category lower than the offense attempted (a state-jail-felony attempt is a Class A misdemeanor). Exception effective Sept. 1, 2025: attempted capital murder of a peace officer is not reduced — it's a 1st degree felony with a 25-year minimum and no parole eligibility under the standard rules.

Criminal Conspiracy
§15.02
−1 category

With intent that a felony be committed, agreeing with one or more persons that they (or one of them) will engage in conduct constituting the offense, plus an overt act by any conspirator in pursuance of the agreement. The overt act itself can be nothing more than preparation.

Punished one category lower than the most serious felony object of the conspiracy.

Criminal Solicitation
§15.03
F1 / F2

With intent that a capital felony or 1st degree felony be committed, requesting, commanding, or attempting to induce another to engage in conduct that would constitute that felony. A conviction requires corroboration — it can't rest on the uncorroborated word of the person solicited.

Solicitation of a capital felony: 1st degree felony. Solicitation of a 1st degree felony: 2nd degree felony.

Criminal Solicitation of a Minor
§15.031
−1 category

With intent that certain listed offenses be committed, requesting, commanding, or attempting to induce a minor (under 17) to engage in conduct that would constitute the offense or make the minor a party to it. Unlike §15.03, this reaches felonies below the 1st-degree/capital threshold.

Generally one category lower than the felony solicited (e.g., soliciting a 2nd degree felony is a 3rd degree felony), except that soliciting certain serious offenses (capital murder, trafficking, specified sexual offenses) is itself a 1st degree felony regardless of the underlying grade.

Renunciation Defense
§15.04

An affirmative defense to attempt, conspiracy, or solicitation if the actor voluntarily and completely renounced the criminal objective — by abandoning the conduct, or by taking further affirmative action that prevented the offense — and the renunciation wasn't just motivated by a fear of getting caught or a decision to postpone or redirect the crime.

Criminal Instruments & Unlawful Interception
Ch. 16
SJ / F2

Covers possession of criminal instruments (something specially made or adapted for criminal use), unlawful interception of wire, oral, or electronic communications, and unlawful installation of a tracking device on a motor vehicle without the owner's/operator's consent.

Possession of a criminal instrument: state jail felony. Unlawful interception: 2nd degree felony. Unlawful tracking device installation: state jail felony.

TITLE 5

Offenses Against the Person

Capital Murder
§19.03
Capital

Murder plus an aggravating factor — e.g. victim is a peace officer/firefighter on duty, multiple victims, murder for hire, victim under 10, murder during kidnapping/robbery/burglary/sexual assault/arson, or while incarcerated.

Life without parole, or death if the State seeks it (juveniles/certain offenders excluded from LWOP by law).

Murder
§19.02
F1 / F2

Intentionally or knowingly causing death, or intending serious bodily injury and committing an act clearly dangerous to human life that causes death, or causing death during a felony.

1st degree felony. Reduced to 2nd degree felony if caused under the immediate influence of sudden passion arising from an adequate cause.

Manslaughter
§19.04
Felony 2

Recklessly causing the death of an individual.

2nd degree felony.

Criminally Negligent Homicide
§19.05
State Jail

Causing a death through criminal negligence (failure to perceive a substantial and unjustifiable risk).

State jail felony.

Kidnapping / Aggravated Kidnapping
§20.03 / §20.04
F3 / F1 / F2

Kidnapping = intentionally or knowingly abducting another. Aggravated = abduction plus intent to hold for ransom, use as a shield/hostage, facilitate a felony, terrorize, or inflict injury/sexual abuse.

Kidnapping: 3rd degree felony. Aggravated Kidnapping: 1st degree felony; drops to 2nd degree if the actor voluntarily releases the victim in a safe place.

Leaving a Child in a Vehicle
§22.10
Class C

Intentionally or knowingly leaving a child younger than 7 alone in a motor vehicle for longer than five minutes, with no one 14 or older attending them in the vehicle.

Class C misdemeanor.

Unlawful Restraint
§20.02
A / SJ / F3 / F2

Intentionally or knowingly restricting another's movement without consent so as to interfere substantially with their liberty, by moving or confining them.

Base: Class A misdemeanor.

EnhancementsState jail felony if victim is under 17. 3rd degree felony if committed recklessly exposing the victim to substantial risk of serious bodily injury, against a public servant on duty, or by an actor already in custody/civil commitment. 2nd degree felony if the victim is a peace officer or judge on duty (this overrides the public-servant 3rd degree tier).
Assault
§22.01
C / A / F3 / F2

Intentionally, knowingly, or recklessly causing bodily injury to another; threatening another with imminent bodily injury; or causing offensive/provocative physical contact.

Threat or offensive contact only: Class C. Causing bodily injury: Class A.

Enhancements Class B if against a sports participant (by a non-participant), or (as a separate Class A tier) if the offensive-contact/threat form targets a pregnant individual to force an abortion. 3rd degree felony if against: a public servant on duty; a security officer, process server, or emergency-services worker on duty; hospital personnel on hospital property; a utility employee/agent on duty; a pregnant individual (to force an abortion, or simply known to be pregnant); or a family/household member with a prior family-violence conviction. 2nd degree felony if by strangulation/suffocation, or against a peace officer/judge on duty; strangulation against a family member with a prior conviction is also 2nd degree. Family-violence findings attach collateral consequences at any grade; repeated bodily-injury assaults against a family member can also be charged as Continuous Family Violence (§25.11, 3rd degree felony).
Indecent Assault
§22.012
A / SJ / F3 / F2

Without consent and with intent to arouse or gratify sexual desire, touching another's anus, breast, or genitals (or causing such contact), exposing another's intimate areas, or causing contact with bodily fluids. Added in 2019 to cover conduct not otherwise reaching the sexual assault threshold.

Base: Class A misdemeanor. State jail felony with a qualifying prior conviction, or if committed by a health/mental-health provider beyond the scope of treatment. 3rd degree felony with a prior provider-abuse conviction. 2nd degree felony if the victim is elderly or disabled.

Aggravated Assault
§22.02
F2 / F1

Assault that causes serious bodily injury, or in which the actor uses or exhibits a deadly weapon.

Base: 2nd degree felony.

EnhancementsBecomes a 1st degree felony if committed against a public servant/informant/witness in retaliation, a process server or security officer on duty, during a drive-by shooting, as part of a mass shooting (added 2025), or — with a deadly weapon — causing serious bodily injury to a family/household member or a traumatic brain/spine injury resulting in a persistent vegetative state or irreversible paralysis (added 2025).
Sexual Assault / Aggravated Sexual Assault
§22.011 / §22.021
F2 / F1

Sexual Assault = intentionally or knowingly causing penetration/contact without consent. Aggravated = adds a deadly weapon, serious bodily injury, threats, a victim under 6 (or under 14 with aggravating factors), or an elderly/disabled victim.

Sexual Assault: 2nd degree felony (1st degree if the victim is someone the actor was legally barred from marrying). Aggravated Sexual Assault: 1st degree felony, with a 25-year minimum if the victim is under 6, or under 14 with threats/serious bodily injury/deadly weapon.

Injury to a Child, Elderly, or Disabled Individual
§22.04
SJ / F3 / F2 / F1

Intentionally, knowingly, recklessly, or negligently causing bodily injury, serious bodily injury, or serious mental deficiency/impairment to a child, elderly, or disabled person.

Criminal negligence causing bodily injury: state jail felony. Reckless bodily injury: state jail; reckless serious bodily injury: 2nd degree. Intentional/knowing bodily injury: 3rd degree. Intentional/knowing serious bodily injury: 1st degree.

Deadly Conduct
§22.05
A / F3

Recklessly engaging in conduct that places another in imminent danger of serious bodily injury.

Base: Class A misdemeanor. 3rd degree felony if committed by discharging a firearm at or in the direction of a person, habitation, building, or vehicle.

Terroristic Threat
§22.07
B / A / SJ / F3

Threatening violence with intent to place a person in fear, cause an emergency response, interrupt public services, or influence a government's conduct.

Placing a person in fear / triggering an emergency response: Class B. Family-violence context or threats against public servants/public places: Class A. Causing $1,500+ pecuniary loss or targeting a peace officer/judge: state jail felony. Impairing public services or influencing government conduct: 3rd degree felony.

TITLE 6

Offenses Against the Family

Continuous Family Violence
§25.11
Felony 3

Engaging in conduct that constitutes assault causing bodily injury two or more times within a 12-month period against a family/household member or dating partner.

3rd degree felony.

Violation of a Protective Order
§25.07
A / SJ / F3

Knowingly violating the terms of a protective order or magistrate's order in a family violence, child abuse/neglect, sexual assault/abuse, indecent assault, stalking, or trafficking case.

Base: Class A misdemeanor.

EnhancementsState jail felony if the violated order was issued following the defendant's conviction (or deferred adjudication) for an offense against that same victim, or if certain violations (e.g., going near the victim, threatening/harassing contact) were committed while possessing a deadly weapon. 3rd degree felony if the person has two or more prior convictions under this section (or a combination of this section and §25.072), or violated the order by committing assault or stalking.
Interference With Child Custody
§25.03
State Jail

Taking or retaining a child in violation of a custody judgment or in a manner that deprives a lawful custodian of custody.

State jail felony.

Criminal Nonsupport
§25.05
State Jail

Intentionally or knowingly failing to provide support for one's dependent child.

State jail felony.

TITLE 7

Offenses Against Property

Arson (Intentional)
§28.02(a), (d)
F2 / F1

Starting a fire or causing an explosion with intent to destroy or damage vegetation/property on open-space land, or a habitation, building, or other property, without the owner's consent (or with the intent to defraud an insurer, or being reckless about endangering life/property).

Base: 2nd degree felony. 1st degree felony if bodily injury or death resulted, or the property was a habitation or a place of assembly or worship.

Arson During Drug Manufacturing
§28.02(a-1), (e)
SJ / F3

Recklessly (not intentionally) starting a fire or causing an explosion while manufacturing or attempting to manufacture a controlled substance, where the fire or explosion damages a building, habitation, or vehicle.

Base: state jail felony, rising to a 3rd degree felony if bodily injury or death resulted.

Reckless Arson (Collateral Damage/Injury)
§28.02(a-2), (f)
State Jail

Intentionally starting a fire or causing an explosion (e.g., a permitted or otherwise lawful burn) but in doing so recklessly damaging or destroying another's building, or recklessly causing another person to suffer bodily injury or death.

State jail felony.

Criminal Mischief
§28.03
C→F1

Intentionally or knowingly damaging, destroying, or tampering with another's property without consent.

Graded entirely by pecuniary loss — same value ladder as Theft below: Class C (under $100) up through 1st degree felony ($300,000+). Loss of $30,000+ on a place of worship, cemetery, monument, or school can bump the grade.

Robbery
§29.02
Felony 2

While committing theft, intentionally, knowingly, or recklessly causing bodily injury, or threatening/placing another in fear of imminent bodily injury or death.

2nd degree felony.

Aggravated Robbery
§29.03
Felony 1

Robbery that causes serious bodily injury, uses or exhibits a deadly weapon, or is committed against a person 65 or older or a disabled person.

1st degree felony.

Burglary
§30.02
SJ / F2 / F1

Entering a habitation or building not open to the public, without consent, with intent to commit a felony, theft, or assault (or entering/remaining and committing/attempting one of those).

Building other than a habitation: state jail felony. Habitation: 2nd degree felony. Habitation with intent to commit a felony other than theft: 1st degree felony.

Niche enhancements3rd degree felony if a building other than a habitation, where the building is a pharmacy or other facility that generally stores controlled substances and the intent was to steal a controlled substance; also 3rd degree if committed in connection with a human-smuggling offense.
Burglary of a Vehicle
§30.04
A / SJ

Breaking into or entering a vehicle, or any part of one, without consent, with intent to commit a felony or theft.

Base: Class A misdemeanor.

EnhancementsOne prior conviction under this section raises the minimum confinement to 6 months (still a Class A). Two or more priors, or the vehicle/part entered being a rail car, makes it a state jail felony. A niche 3rd degree felony tier also applies to certain wholesale-pharmaceutical-vehicle break-ins with intent to steal a controlled substance.
Criminal Trespass
§30.05
C / B / A / F3

Entering or remaining on property or in a building without consent after notice to depart (posted sign, fencing, or verbal warning).

Base: Class B misdemeanor.

EnhancementsClass C if on agricultural land within 100 feet of the boundary, or on residential land within 100 feet of a protected freshwater area. Class A if in a habitation, on a critical-infrastructure facility, at a residential treatment center, on higher-ed property with a qualifying prior, or while carrying a deadly weapon. 3rd degree felony if committed in the course of a human-smuggling offense. (A separate, narrower Class C/A tier applies to trespass by a licensed handgun holder ignoring a firearm-specific no-entry sign.)
Theft
§31.03
C→F1

Unlawfully appropriating property with intent to deprive the owner of it.

Graded by value of the property stolen:

<$100 → Class C  |  $100–$750 → Class B  |  $750–$2,500 → Class A  |  $2,500–$30,000 → State Jail Felony  |  $30,000–$150,000 → Felony 3  |  $150,000–$300,000 → Felony 2  |  $300,000+ → Felony 1

EnhancementsRegardless of value: theft of a firearm, a corpse/grave item, or official election material is at least a state jail felony. Two-or-more prior theft convictions bump a low-value theft up a grade. Victim being elderly, a nonprofit, or the theft involving a fire-alarm/retail-detector defeat device raises the grade by one level.
Unauthorized Use of a Vehicle
§31.07
State Jail

Intentionally or knowingly operating another's vehicle without the owner's effective consent (the Texas "joyriding" statute — no intent to permanently deprive required).

State jail felony.

Credit/Debit Card Abuse
§32.31
State Jail

Using, possessing, or selling another's credit or debit card without consent, or with intent to fraudulently present an expired/revoked/fictitious card.

State jail felony (raised one grade if the victim is elderly).

Fraudulent Use/Possession of Identifying Information
§32.51
State Jail+

Obtaining, possessing, transferring, or using another person's identifying information without consent and with intent to harm or defraud.

State jail felony minimum; grade rises with the number of items of identifying information involved (up to 1st degree for 50+), and one grade higher if the victim is elderly.

TITLE 8

Offenses Against Public Administration

Resisting Arrest, Search, or Transportation
§38.03
A / F3

Using force against a peace officer or another to prevent a lawful arrest, search, or transport. (Knowledge that the person is a peace officer is required, but resistance to a merely unlawful arrest is not a defense.)

Base: Class A misdemeanor.

Enhancement3rd degree felony if the actor uses a deadly weapon to resist.
Evading Arrest or Detention
§38.04
A / SJ / F3 / F2

Intentionally fleeing from a person the actor knows is a peace officer lawfully attempting to arrest or detain them.

Base: Class A misdemeanor.

EnhancementsState jail felony if the actor has a prior conviction under this section, or uses a vehicle/watercraft while fleeing (first offense). 3rd degree felony if the actor uses a vehicle/watercraft while fleeing and has a prior conviction, if the flight causes serious bodily injury to another, if a tire-deflation device is used against the officer, or if committed in the course of a human-smuggling offense. 2nd degree felony if another person dies as a direct result, or suffers serious bodily injury from the actor's own use of a tire-deflation device.
Failure to Identify
§38.02
C / B / A

Intentionally refusing to give your name, address, or date of birth to a peace officer after a lawful arrest, or intentionally giving false/fictitious information to an officer who has lawfully arrested or detained you, or who you know is investigating a crime and you're a witness.

Refusing to give info after a lawful arrest: Class C misdemeanor. Giving false information (whether arrested, detained, or as a witness): Class B misdemeanor.

EnhancementIf the actor is a fugitive from justice (has an outstanding arrest warrant), each tier is raised one level: refusal becomes a Class B, and false information becomes a Class A misdemeanor.
Tampering With or Fabricating Physical Evidence
§37.09
F3 / F2 / A

Knowing an investigation is pending, altering, destroying, or concealing evidence, or fabricating/presenting false evidence, with intent to impair its availability. A related subsection covers knowing an offense occurred and tampering with evidence of it, or observing a human corpse under suspicious circumstances and failing to report it.

3rd degree felony (2nd degree if the evidence altered/concealed is a human corpse). Simply failing to report an observed corpse under suspicious circumstances, without more, is a Class A misdemeanor.

Perjury / Aggravated Perjury
§37.02 / §37.03
A / F3

Perjury = making a false statement under oath with intent to deceive. Aggravated = perjury made during or in connection with an official proceeding and material to the proceeding.

Perjury: Class A misdemeanor. Aggravated Perjury: 3rd degree felony.

Bail Jumping and Failure to Appear
§38.10
C / A / F3

Intentionally or knowingly failing to appear in accordance with the terms of release after being lawfully released on bail, with intent to avoid prosecution.

Same grade as the offense for which the person was released, capped: fine-only underlying offense → Class C; misdemeanor → Class A; felony → 3rd degree felony.

TITLE 9

Offenses Against Public Order & Decency

Disorderly Conduct
§42.01
C / B

A broad statute covering offensive language, fighting words, obscene gestures, unreasonable noise, exposing oneself, or displaying a firearm to alarm.

Base: Class C misdemeanor.

EnhancementClass B if committed by discharging a firearm in certain public places, or by displaying a firearm/deadly weapon in public in a manner calculated to alarm.
Riot
§42.02
B / +1

Assembling with 7+ people and engaging in conduct that creates an immediate danger of property damage/injury, substantially obstructs law enforcement, or by force interferes with a government function.

Base: Class B misdemeanor for mere participation.

EnhancementAn offense actually committed in furtherance of the riot's purpose (or reasonably foreseeable from it) is punished one grade higher than mere participation.
Harassment
§42.07
B / A / SJ / F3

Intending to annoy, alarm, abuse, torment, or embarrass another via repeated calls, electronic communications, threats, or obscene suggestions.

Base: Class B misdemeanor.

EnhancementsClass A if: the actor has a prior conviction under this section; the offense was electronic harassment of a child under 18 intending to cause suicide or self-harm; the actor previously violated a related restraining order/injunction; or (added 2025) the victim is known to be a utility employee/agent or a court employee acting in that role. State jail felony if against a court employee and the actor has a prior conviction under this section. 3rd degree felony if against a judge and the actor has a prior conviction under this section.
Stalking
§42.072
F3 / F2

Engaging in repeated conduct directed at a specific person that the actor knows or should know would cause a reasonable person to fear bodily injury, death, or property damage.

Base: 3rd degree felony, rising to 2nd degree felony if the actor has a prior conviction for stalking (Texas or a substantially similar out-of-state/tribal/federal offense).

Prostitution (Selling)
§43.02
B / A / SJ

Knowingly offering or agreeing to receive a fee from another to engage in sexual conduct. A 2021 reform split this offense from the buyer's side (now its own statute, below) and repealed the old flat "engaging in prostitution" language.

Base: Class B misdemeanor. Class A misdemeanor with 1–2 prior convictions under this section. State jail felony with 3+ priors, or if the actor is a peace officer, security officer, or public school employee (added 2023).

Solicitation of Prostitution (Buying)
§43.021
SJ / F3 / F2

Knowingly offering or agreeing to pay a fee to another person for the purpose of engaging in sexual conduct with that person or another. Added in 2021 — Texas was the first state to make buying sex a felony on the first offense.

Base: state jail felony. 3rd degree felony with a prior conviction under this section (or under the old pre-2021 §43.02(b)). 2nd degree felony if the person solicited is under 18 — regardless of whether the actor knew or was told the person's real age.

TITLE 10

Offenses Against Public Health, Safety & Morals

Unlawful Carrying Weapons (UCW)
§46.02
A / F3 / F2

Since "constitutional carry" took effect Sept. 1, 2021, adults 21+ who may lawfully possess a handgun generally don't need a license to carry openly or concealed. §46.02 now criminalizes carrying by narrower categories: a person under 21, a person carrying while engaged in other criminal activity (besides a Class C traffic/boating offense), a person intoxicated, a member of a criminal street gang, or a person already prohibited from possessing a firearm under §46.04.

Base: Class A misdemeanor.

Enhancements3rd degree felony if carried into a place weapons are prohibited under §46.03 (bars/51% establishments, schools, polling places, government meetings, etc.), or if the actor was already prohibited from possessing a firearm under §46.04(b) or (c) (family-violence or protective-order basis). 2nd degree felony, with a 5-year minimum, if the actor was prohibited under §46.04(a) (felon status). Any Chapter 46 weapons offense is also bumped one category higher if committed knowingly within 300 feet of a school or a UIL event (Weapon-Free School Zone, §46.11).
Unlawful Possession of Firearm by Felon
§46.04
A / F3

A convicted felon possessing a firearm before the 5th anniversary of release from confinement/supervision, or at any other location besides their own premises after that period, or possessing while subject to a family-violence protective order.

Base: 3rd degree felony. Class A misdemeanor if the disqualifying basis is a family-violence conviction or an active protective order (rather than felon status).

Prohibited Weapons
§46.05
F3 / SJ

Possessing, manufacturing, transporting, or selling specific prohibited devices — explosive weapons (including improvised explosive devices, added 2017), machine guns, armor-piercing ammunition, chemical dispensing devices, zip guns, and tire deflation devices.

Most listed items: 3rd degree felony. Tire deflation devices specifically: state jail felony.

Note — items removed from this listKnuckles were removed from this offense entirely in 2019 (no longer a crime under this section). Firearm silencers were removed in 2021. Short-barrel firearms were removed as of September 1, 2025. None of these three currently violate §46.05.
Driving While Intoxicated (DWI)
§49.04
B / A / F3

Operating a motor vehicle in a public place while intoxicated (no normal use of mental/physical faculties, or BAC ≥ 0.08).

Base (1st offense): Class B misdemeanor, minimum 72-hour confinement (6 days if open container present).

EnhancementsClass A if there is one prior DWI-type conviction, or if BAC is 0.15+ on the instant offense (30-day minimum). 3rd degree felony with two or more prior convictions. DWI with a passenger under 15 is charged separately as a state jail felony (§49.045), regardless of prior record.
Intoxication Assault
§49.07
Felony 3

By accident or mistake, causing serious bodily injury to another while operating a vehicle, aircraft, watercraft, or amusement ride while intoxicated.

3rd degree felony; enhanced to 2nd degree felony if the victim is a peace officer, firefighter, or EMS personnel (on or off duty).

Intoxication Manslaughter
§49.08
Felony 2

By accident or mistake, causing the death of another while operating a vehicle, aircraft, watercraft, or amusement ride while intoxicated.

2nd degree felony; enhanced to 1st degree felony if the victim is a peace officer, firefighter, or EMS personnel.

Public Intoxication
§49.02
Class C

Appearing in a public place while so intoxicated as to be a danger to oneself or others.

Class C misdemeanor. (Three prior convictions for Public Intoxication and/or Disorderly Conduct within 24 months can enhance to a Class B under §12.43(c).)

TITLE 11

Organized Crime

Engaging in Organized Criminal Activity
§71.02
+1 category

Committing (or conspiring to commit) a listed offense with intent to establish, maintain, or participate in a combination (3+ people collaborating in ongoing criminal activity) or as a member of a criminal street gang. Covers a long list of predicate offenses — murder, robbery, aggravated assault, theft, burglary, drug offenses, human trafficking, fraud, and more.

Punished one category higher than the most serious predicate offense listed (e.g., an underlying 3rd degree felony becomes a 2nd degree felony). A deadly weapon finding pushes it a further category higher.

Coercing, Inducing, or Soliciting Gang Membership
§71.022
Felony 2

Knowingly causing, enabling, encouraging, recruiting, or soliciting another to join a criminal street gang or foreign terrorist organization that requires committing a Class A misdemeanor or felony as a condition of membership. A separate, more serious provision covers threatening or injuring a child or the child's family to coerce the child into gang activity.

2nd degree felony (the child-coercion provision carries its own elevated range).

Directing Activities of Criminal Street Gangs or Foreign Terrorist Organizations
§71.023
Felony 1

Knowingly financing, directing, or supervising the activities of a criminal street gang or (as of a 2023 amendment) a foreign terrorist organization, including a drug cartel operating partly outside the U.S.

1st degree felony, with a 25-year minimum sentence.

Violation of Court Order Enjoining Organized Criminal Activity
§71.021

Knowingly violating a civil injunction obtained against a criminal street gang or combination that restrains specific activity.

Gang-Free Zones
§71.028

Certain drug and weapons offenses committed by a criminal street gang member within 1,000 feet of a school, youth center, playground, or similar designated area can be enhanced, similar in concept to the weapon-free school zone enhancement in Title 10.

TITLE 12

Terrorism

Added to the Penal Code by the 86th Legislature (2019) as Chapter 72 — before that, Title 11 covered organized crime alone. Title 11 was formally renamed "Organized Crime and Terrorism" and this new title/chapter was carved out to address conduct in support of terrorism specifically.
Providing Material Support or Resources to Facilitate Terrorism
Ch. 72
+1 category

Knowingly providing material support or resources (property, services, or personnel) to another, knowing or intending they'll be used to plan, prepare, or carry out an act that would constitute one of a list of serious predicate offenses — including felonies under the fraud/computer-crime chapters, money laundering, unlawful drug manufacture or distribution, witness tampering, evidence tampering, tampering with a government record, or hindering apprehension.

Punished one category higher than the most serious predicate offense supported — the same escalation structure used in the Title 11 organized-crime statute, but aimed at material support for terrorism rather than gang activity.

Key Definitions
§72.01

Defines "foreign terrorist organization" by reference to the federal designation process under 8 U.S.C. §1189, and defines "material support or resources" broadly to include real and personal property and services rendered.

Study note: This now spans all twelve Penal Code titles, but within each title it's still the offenses and provisions most commonly tested for patrol-level TCOLE prep, not a line-by-line reproduction of the whole code — some of Titles 8–9's narrower administrative and decency offenses are condensed, and all Health & Safety Code drug offenses (a separate code from the Penal Code entirely) are left out. The Legislature amends penalty ranges and enhancements almost every regular session — most recently expanding assault enhancements for utility and hospital workers in 2023 and 2025, and adding Title 12 (Terrorism) itself in 2019 — so cross-check anything you're unsure about against the current statute or your academy's TCOLE study guide before test day.
